Everyone in this field has a cursed object story. Most of them end badly because the person holding the object didn't do their homework. That's not a curse problem — that's a preparation problem.

I've carried a cursed compass since 1991. It's never once pointed north. It has, however, pointed me toward three portal sites, one subterranean archive, and a warehouse in Bruges that I am still not allowed to discuss. The compass doesn't do what it's supposed to. It does something better.

That's the thing about cursed artifacts that nobody tells you.

## The Taxonomy of Curses

Not all curses are punishments. A significant portion — I'd estimate 40%, based on my own catalog — are protection mechanisms. Ancient engineers didn't have locks the way we do. They had consequences. The curse isn't the point; the barrier is.

Once you understand that, cursed objects stop being threats and start being puzzles. The question shifts from "how do I avoid this" to "what is this protecting, and from whom."

## What to Look For

Surface corrosion that follows a pattern. Inscriptions on the interior face rather than the exterior — that's always intentional. Weight distribution that doesn't match the material. These are tells. An object that's been cursed with purpose has a kind of internal logic to it, and that logic is readable if you know the tradition it came from.

I once identified a cursed reliquary in under three minutes because the weight was wrong. Hollow where it should have been solid. Whatever was inside wasn't the relic on the label.

It wasn't.

## The Ones You Leave Alone

There are cursed objects I won't touch. I keep a list. They're characterized by curses that don't protect anything — they just punish contact. No logic, no structure, no puzzle to solve. Something went wrong in the original working, or the intent was purely hostile. These exist, and they're not interesting. They're just dangerous.

Knowing the difference between a protection curse and a spite curse has saved my life more than once.

## Conclusion

Cursed artifacts are not bad luck. They're information. They tell you something mattered enough to protect this way, that someone knew techniques worth encoding into an object, that there's a tradition behind this thing with rules you can learn.

Do your research. Respect the object. And if the compass stops pointing north, pay attention to where it's pointing instead.
