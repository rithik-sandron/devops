publishedAt: '2024-04-09'

---

For a long time I thought there was one. One portal, one site, one anomaly beneath the sand that I'd stumbled into in 1987 and spent the next decade trying to understand. I was wrong. There are at least four. Possibly more that I haven't found yet, or haven't recognized for what they are.

This is what I know. I'm writing it down because I won't be doing this forever, and someone else should have a head start.

## The Original Site

Roughly 200 kilometers southwest of where any map says anything interesting is. The entrance inscription is seventeen glyphs in a writing system I've never been able to fully classify — it borrows from at least three traditions and may predate all of them. The portal itself is not a door. It's more like a pressure differential that shouldn't exist at that depth. You feel it before you see anything.

I've been through it four times. Each time, the elapsed time on the surface was different from what I experienced on the other side. Not dramatically. Minutes, not years. But consistently asymmetric.

## The Second Site

Found in 2001, following a set of coordinates I decoded from an Elamite tablet that had been misclassified as a trade record in a private collection. The coordinates were accurate to within fifty meters, which means whoever left them had surveyed the site with precision instruments or something functionally equivalent.

This one is sealed. I haven't been able to open it. The inscription above the entrance is a warning, not an invitation, and I've learned to respect the distinction.

## What Connects Them

Geology first: both sites sit on the same deep aquifer system. The water table does something strange in this region that conventional hydrology doesn't fully account for. I think the portals are related to that, though I can't yet say how.

Second: both sites were known. Not recently — anciently. There are references in texts that scholars have dismissed as mythological that describe, with uncomfortable specificity, locations matching both sites. The mythology is a map. It usually is.

## What I Still Don't Know

Why 1987. I've never been able to determine whether the portal was newly active when I found it, or whether I found it because it was newly active, or whether the timing was coincidence. I don't believe in coincidence, which means I still have an open question I haven't answered in nearly forty years.

That bothers me more than almost anything else about this.

## Conclusion

I'm not writing this to be believed. I'm writing it so that when someone else finds these sites — and someone will — they have context I didn't have. The eleven years I spent assuming there was only one portal was eleven years of looking at evidence incorrectly.

Don't make the same assumption. The Sahara is larger than it appears on any map, and deeper than any map shows at all.
