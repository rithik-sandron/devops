People think dead languages are for academics. Dusty libraries, tweed jackets, grant applications. They're wrong. Dead languages are the most practical skill I've ever picked up, and I've picked up a lot of skills under circumstances I can't legally describe.

Here's the thing about a language nobody speaks anymore — nobody can intercept it casually. Your communications don't need encryption if the reader doesn't exist.

## Elamite Saved My Life in 2003

I'm not going to get into the full story. What I will say is that a clay tablet I'd been carrying as a paperweight for six months turned out to contain coordinates, and the only reason I could read them is because I'd spent a winter in a safe house outside Tbilisi with nothing but a cracked Elamite grammar and too much time.

The syndicate had three linguists on staff. None of them were trained in Elamite. I had a twelve-hour head start.

## The Rosetta Effect

Once you learn one dead language, the next one comes faster. They cross-contaminate in useful ways. Akkadian opens up Sumerian. Classical Ethiopic unlocks a whole corridor of East African inscription traditions that most institutions don't even have catalogued yet.

I currently read seven. I'm working on an eighth, though my contact in Kyoto insists it's not technically a language so much as a "notation system for spatial coordinates encoded as poetry." Semantics. I'll have it by spring.

## What You Actually Need to Start

You don't need a university. You need three things: a good grammar (PDF, stolen, or otherwise), a corpus of inscriptions to practice against, and a reason to care. The reason is important. Abstract study fades. Attach it to something real — an artifact, a location, a question you actually need answered — and it sticks.

The portal beneath the Sahara had a seventeen-glyph warning inscription above the entrance. I read it in under a minute. That minute is the reason I'm still here.

## Conclusion

Learn a dead language. Pick one that connects to the region you're working in, or the period you're investigating, or the organization you're trying to stay ahead of. Put in the hours before you need it, not during.

You will need it. Trust me on that.
